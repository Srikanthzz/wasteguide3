Biomedical waste (BMW) refers to waste generated from healthcare facilities, medical research institutions, and laboratories, including:

Sources

1. Infectious waste: Pathological, microbiological, and contaminated materials.
2. Anatomical waste: Human tissues, organs, and body parts.
3. Pharmaceutical waste: Expired or contaminated medications.
4. Chemical waste: Laboratory chemicals and reagents.
5. Radioactive waste: Radioactive materials and contaminated equipment.

10 Tips to Manage Biomedical Waste

Segregation and Storage

1. Color-code and label: Use standardized color-coding and labeling for different types of BMW.
2. Separate storage: Store BMW separately from non-hazardous waste.
3. Leak-proof containers: Use puncture-resistant, leak-proof containers.

Handling and Transportation

1. Trained personnel: Ensure staff handling BMW are trained and equipped with personal protective equipment (PPE).
2. Secure transportation: Use designated vehicles for BMW transportation.

Treatment and Disposal

1. Incineration: Use high-temperature incineration for infectious and anatomical waste.
2. Autoclaving: Use steam sterilization for non-infectious waste.
3. Chemical disinfection: Disinfect chemical waste before disposal.

Regulatory Compliance

1. Comply with regulations: Familiarize yourself with local, national, and international regulations (e.g., WHO, EPA, OSHA).
2. Maintain records: Document generation, storage, treatment, and disposal activities.


Best Practices

1. Implement waste minimization strategies.
2. Use eco-friendly packaging.
3. Regularly inspect storage facilities.
4. Develop emergency response plans.
5. Conduct regular staff training.
