Indore, Madhya Pradesh, India, has achieved remarkable success in waste management, earning recognition globally. Here's their inspiring story:

Key Achievements

1. Zero-Waste City: Indore became India's first city to achieve 100% waste segregation and processing.
2. 100% Door-to-Door Collection: Waste collected from every household daily.
3. Three-Color Code: Separate bins for organic (green), inorganic (blue), and hazardous (red) waste.
4. Waste-to-Compost: 500 tons of organic waste converted into compost daily.
5. Waste-to-Energy: 15 MW electricity generated from waste.

Innovative Strategies

1. Public-Private Partnership (PPP): Collaboration between Indore Municipal Corporation (IMC) and private companies.
2. Decentralized Waste Management: Waste processed at local levels, reducing transportation costs.
3. Community Engagement: Awareness campaigns, rallies, and workshops involving citizens, schools, and NGOs.
4. Incentivizing Waste Segregation: Rewards for citizens segregating waste correctly.
5. Technology Integration: GPS-enabled waste collection vehicles and online monitoring systems.

Impact

1. Reduced Waste: 90% reduction in waste dumped in landfills.
2. Improved Sanitation: Cleaner streets, reduced odors, and minimized pest infestations.
3. Increased Recycling: 60% of waste recycled or composted.
4. Job Creation: Employment opportunities for 1,500 waste workers.
5. Economic Benefits: Savings of ₹50 crores (~ $6.7 million USD) annually.

Awards and Recognition

1. Cleanest City in India (2017-2020): Swachh Survekshan Awards.
2. Global Waste Management Award (2018): International Solid Waste Association.
3. UN Environment Programme's (2019): "Beating Plastic Pollution" award.

Key Players

1. Indore Municipal Corporation (IMC)
2. Madhya Pradesh Government
3. Private partners (e.g., A2Z Waste Management)
4. NGOs (e.g., Swachh Bharat Abhiyan)
5. Community groups

Navjeevan Vihar colony in New Delhi has achieved remarkable success as a zero-waste colony. This transformation began in 2017 when the Municipal Corporation of Delhi visited the colony to raise awareness about waste management ¹.

The residents, led by Dr. Bhairavi Hiremath, implemented effective waste management techniques. They started segregating waste into categories, including wet waste, garden scraps, plastic waste, and pre-loved clothes. The wet waste was converted into compost, reducing the amount of waste sent to landfills.

Key Achievements:
- 10,000 kg of compost produced annually from wet waste ¹
- 600,000 kg of waste diverted from landfill sites ¹
- Nine rainwater harvesting systems built around the colony ¹

The colony's success inspired the creation of the Vikalp Stores project, which aimed to reduce single-use plastic bags. The project introduced reusable cloth bags, available for a refundable deposit of ₹20. This initiative expanded to 750 stores across Delhi, promoting sustainable practices ¹.
